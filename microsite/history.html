History & Legend

The Legend of
Petit Jean

The Story

A Love Story Written in Stone

High atop an Arkansas mountain, where cedar bluffs meet endless sky, rests a grave — and with it, one of the most enduring legends of the American South.

The story of Petit Jean is the story of a young French woman whose devotion to the man she loved carried her halfway across the world — disguised as a cabin boy — to a wilderness she would never leave.

"She crossed an ocean dressed as a boy, climbed an untamed mountain, and left her name upon it forever."

— Arkansas State Parks
Timeline

From Legend to Legacy

⚓
Early 1700s
A Daring Disguise

A young French woman learned that her fiancé had been assigned a voyage to the New World. Unwilling to be left behind, she cut her hair, donned sailor's clothes, and boarded the ship under the name "Petit Jean" — "Little John."

⛵
The Voyage
Crossing the Atlantic

For months she maintained her disguise aboard the vessel. None of the crew — not even her fiancé — suspected the quiet, hardworking cabin boy was the woman he had left behind in France.

🌿
Arrival in the New World
The Arkansas Wilderness

The expedition made its way inland up the Arkansas River. The mountain that rose dramatically above the valley captivated the explorers. They made camp on its summit, surrounded by untouched forest and cedar canyons.

🕊️
The Revelation
Exposed at Last

Weakened by the journey and harsh conditions, the young woman fell gravely ill. On what would be her deathbed, she finally revealed herself to her shocked and heartbroken fiancé. She died shortly after, having kept her secret across an ocean and a continent.

⛰️
Her Legacy
A Mountain Bears Her Name

She was buried atop the mountain — her grave still visible today in the park. The explorers named the peak in her honor: Petit Jean Mountain. Her story passed down through generations, becoming one of Arkansas' most cherished legends.

🌲
1923
Arkansas' First State Park

Nearly two centuries later, the mountain became the site of Arkansas' first state park. The Civilian Conservation Corps later built the park's stone structures, bridges, and trails — all of which still stand today, monuments to New Deal craftsmanship and the enduring legend of Petit Jean.

Visit Her Grave

Pay Your Respects

The grave of Petit Jean is one of the most visited sites in the park. Located near the summit with sweeping views of the Arkansas River Valley, it is a peaceful and moving place — a stone marker bearing only the name of a woman who crossed a world for love.

A short, easy trail leads from the lodge to the overlook where she is buried. The hike takes approximately 20 minutes one way and is suitable for all ages.
